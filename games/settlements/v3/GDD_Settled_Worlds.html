01
GAME OVERVIEW & VISION

Settled Worlds is a browser-based incremental city-builder where each settlement you stabilize becomes a collectible card — and those cards are the building materials for your next, larger settlement. The game is played entirely on an HTML5 Canvas in a pixel-art style.

The central insight is the recursive scale loop: you don't just grow one city — you grow a civilisation of cities, each layer feeding the next. A Hamlet is not merely a bigger Settlement; it is three Settlements fused together, each contributing its unique terrain bonuses, building footprints and sealed passive bonuses.

▶ THE CORE HOOK
Every city you build eventually becomes a permanent collectible artifact — a card in your deck — that you slot into the blueprint of your next, bigger city. This means no playthrough is ever "wasted." Everything compounds.

ELEVATOR PITCH

"Build a settlement. Stabilize it. Seal it. Use it as a tile in something bigger. Repeat forever — but each layer is more complex, more rewarding, and more strategic than the last."

▶ DESIGN NOTE — THE SEAL MOMENT
The moment a player seals a settlement is the most emotionally significant event in the game. It should feel ceremonial — a brief animation, a "card flip" reveal, a rarity fanfare. This is the game's primary dopamine beat and must be polished first.

T3
VILLAGE
3× T2 Hamlet cards

T4
TOWN
3× T3 Village cards

3× T4 Town cards

▶ DESIGN NOTE — ASYMMETRIC REQUIREMENTS
Later: Allow founding a Hamlet from 2 Settlements + 1 special "wildcard" card (obtainable via roguelite events). This creates collection flexibility without breaking the core formula. Also consider allowing higher-tier cards to substitute — e.g. 1 Hamlet card = 3 Settlement cards for founding purposes.

FOUNDING CEREMONY

When a player has enough cards to found a new tier, they enter Founding Mode. The three (or more) settlement cards are displayed as physical tiles. The player drags and arranges them to form the new settlement's base map — the chosen arrangement determines the initial terrain layout of the new settlement. This is a meaningful, tactile moment that makes collection matter spatially.

new_settlement.terrain = merge(cardA.terrain, cardB.terrain, cardC.terrain) new_settlement.starting_bonuses = sum(cardA.sealed_bonus, cardB.sealed_bonus, cardC.sealed_bonus) new_settlement.grid_size = tier_base_size + Σ(card_size_bonus)

SETTLEMENT GENERATION

Each settlement is procedurally generated at the moment of founding. The result is a W×H invisible grid overlaid on a rendered terrain map. The grid is the mechanical layer; the rendered tiles are the visual layer.

GRID SYSTEM

The grid is never drawn as lines. Buildings snap to cells and are visually separated by natural-looking terrain variation. Each cell has one terrain type that affects what can be built there and what bonuses apply.

TIER BASE GRID SIZE MAX CELLS TERRAIN VARIETY
T1 Settlement 8 × 6 48 2 types
T2 Hamlet 12 × 8 96 3 types
T3 Village 16 × 10 160 4 types
T4 Town 20 × 14 280 5 types
T5 City 28 × 18 504 6 types
T6 Metropolis 40 × 26 1040 7 types

TERRAIN TYPES

🌿
GRASSLAND
Default buildable surface. No bonus or penalty. Available at T1.
🌲
FOREST
+wood yield. Must be cleared to build. Clearing yields wood bonus.
💧
WETLAND
+food (fishing/reeds). Cannot be built on without Pier building.
🪨
ROCKY
+stone yield. Slower to build on. Unlocks at T2.
🌾
FERTILE
+2× food production. Only a few cells per map. High rarity modifier.
🌋
VOLCANIC
+minerals/coal. Risky — periodic ember events. Unlocks at T3+.
❄️
TUNDRA
+rare resources. Harsh — food consumption +30%. High-reward rare terrain.

PROCEDURAL GENERATION ALGORITHM

STEP 1 — Noise pass: apply 2D simplex noise → height map STEP 2 — Terrain assign: threshold noise bands → terrain types STEP 3 — Rarity roll: weighted RNG → insert special terrain patches STEP 4 — River pass: trace river from highest point (optional by tier) STEP 5 — Validation: ensure minimum grassland % ≥ 40% (always playable) STEP 6 — Card stamp: record terrain hash → used to generate card art thumbnail

RESOURCE SYSTEM

Resources are unlocked progressively as the player advances through tiers. Each tier introduces one or two new resources, increasing complexity gradually. At any tier, the player must keep their balance in the green — production must exceed consumption — to maintain stability.

T1 — SETTLEMENT RESOURCES

🍖
FOOD
Consumed by all settlers. Produced by Gathering Huts, Berry Bushes. Critical — if zero, settlers abandon jobs.
🍂
FALLEN LEAVES
Fuel for Bonfire. Consumed over time. Produced passively from Forest tiles + Leaf Rakers.
🔥
WARMTH
Non-storable. Generated by Bonfires. If insufficient, settlers work slower and morale drops.
👤
SETTLERS
Population unit. Grow over time when food surplus exists. Cap set by Shelters built.

T2+ ADDITIONAL RESOURCES

TIER UNLOCKED RESOURCE SOURCE USAGE
T2 Hamlet 🪵 Wood Woodcutter's Hut (Forest cells) Building material, fuel
T2 Hamlet 🧺 Goods Workshop Trade, settler happiness
T3 Village 🪨 Stone Quarry (Rocky cells) Advanced buildings
T3 Village ⚔️ Morale Tavern, Festivals Production multiplier
T4 Town 💰 Gold Market, Trade routes Purchases, events
T4 Town 📜 Knowledge Library, Scholars Building upgrades, research
T5 City ⚗️ Essence Monument, rare events Card upgrades, prestige

RESOURCE FLOW MODEL

each_tick (default: 1 second): for each resource R: delta = Σ(producer.rate × worker.efficiency) − Σ(consumer.rate) stockpile[R] = clamp(stockpile[R] + delta, 0, capacity[R]) if stockpile[FOOD] == 0: settlers.work_rate *= 0.5 // starvation penalty if warmth_generated < warmth_required: settlers.work_rate *= 0.7 // cold penalty if stockpile[FOOD] > 0 and time_since_growth > growth_interval: try_spawn_settler()
▶ DESIGN NOTE — FALLEN LEAVES
Fallen Leaves are the T1 "energy" resource — thematically fitting for a woodland micro-settlement. They introduce the concept of a consumable fuel early, teaching the mechanic cheaply. At T2+, they are replaced by Wood as the primary fuel, but Fallen Leaves continue to provide a small ambient warmth bonus if you have forest tiles — rewarding early-game terrain choices.

BUILDING SYSTEM

Buildings are placed on grid cells by the player. Each building occupies one or more cells (defined as a footprint), consumes resources to construct, and either produces resources, enables jobs, or modifies nearby cells.

T1 — SETTLEMENT BUILDINGS

🔥 BONFIRE
T1 · 1×1
Jobs: 1 (Tender)
Produces Warmth. Consumes Fallen Leaves/s. Radius effect on adjacent settlers.
Cost: 5 Wood (or free to place first one)
🛖 SHELTER
T1 · 1×2
Jobs: 0 (passive)
+3 Settler cap. Small food storage bonus. Required before population can grow.
Cost: 8 Wood, 4 Fallen Leaves
🏕️ GATHERING HUT
T1 · 1×1
Jobs: 2 (Gatherers)
+food/s per Gatherer. Bonus if adjacent to Forest or Wetland tile.
Cost: 6 Wood
🍃 LEAF RAKING POST
T1 · 1×1
Jobs: 1 (Raker)
+Fallen Leaves/s. Must be placed adjacent to Forest tile. Only 1 per Forest cluster.
Cost: 4 Wood
🌾 BERRY PATCH
T1 · 2×1
Jobs: 1 (Picker)
Steady passive food. No worker needed but worker doubles output. Needs Grassland or Fertile.
Cost: 3 Wood
📦 CACHE
T1 · 1×1
Jobs: 0 (passive)
+50 Food storage, +30 Fallen Leaves storage. Required to hold enough for night cycles.
Cost: 5 Wood, 2 Stone

BUILDING PLACEMENT RULES

All buildings snap to the invisible grid. Placement is validated against:

RULE DETAIL
Terrain match Some buildings require specific terrain types (e.g. Leaf Raking Post → Forest)
No overlap Multi-cell buildings cannot overlap existing structures or impassable terrain
Adjacency bonuses Some buildings gain bonus when placed next to specific terrain or other buildings
Road access (T3+) Advanced buildings require road connection to the settlement core
Zone limits Each building type has a per-settlement count limit (prevents trivial spam)

BUILDING UPGRADES

Each building has 3 upgrade tiers. Upgrades cost resources and time. Upgrading a building does not change its footprint but visually evolves its pixel art sprite (a key aesthetic reward).

Building.upgrade(level): cost = base_cost × (1.5 ^ level) new_production_rate = base_rate × (1 + 0.4 × level) new_job_slots = base_slots + floor(level / 2) sprite = sprite_sheet[building_type][level]

SETTLER & JOB SYSTEM

Settlers are the core workforce. They do nothing without a job assignment. Their assignment is fully manual — a deliberate choice to make every settler feel meaningful, especially in early tiers when you have only 3–5 people.

SETTLER STATES

STATE
WORKING
Assigned to a building. Contributing their job's output at full rate.
STATE
IDLE
Unassigned. Consumes food but produces nothing. Player should address this.
STATE
HUNGRY
Food stockpile empty. Work rate halved. If prolonged, settler leaves.
STATE
COLD
Warmth insufficient. Work rate reduced. Morale drains. Prioritise Bonfire.
STATE
CONTENT
Fed, warm, assigned. Base rate. Can become "thriving" with morale buffs.

JOB ASSIGNMENT UI CONCEPT

The player clicks a building to open its Job Panel. Empty slot icons appear. The player clicks a slot to assign the next idle settler. Alternatively, a Roster View lists all settlers with their current assignment, with drag-to-reassign support.

SETTLER ATTRIBUTES

Each settler has lightweight procedural attributes (name + 1 trait) to make them feel like characters. Traits provide small bonuses:

TRAIT EFFECT
🌲 Forest-born +20% yield when working Forest-adjacent buildings
🍳 Hearty -15% personal food consumption
🔨 Diligent +10% production at any job
🌙 Night Owl Full efficiency during night tick (normally -15%)
📦 Hoarder Increases the effective capacity of their assigned building's storage
🔥 Flame-keeper Bonfires they tend consume 20% less Fallen Leaves

POPULATION GROWTH

spawn_check (every growth_interval seconds): if (food_surplus > 0 AND shelter_vacancy > 0 AND warmth_sufficient): new_settler = generate_settler(name, trait) settlement.settlers.push(new_settler) growth_interval *= 1.2 // each birth takes slightly longer than last
▶ IDLE DESIGN NOTE — AUTO-ASSIGN
A late-game unlock called "Elder's Wisdom" enables auto-assign, which fills empty job slots with idle settlers using a priority queue. This is the idle layer — once a settlement is stable, you can walk away. The resource engine keeps ticking. This is intentional: the game should reward both active players (who min-max job assignments) and passive players (who use auto-assign and check in later).
09
STABILITY & SEALING MECHANIC

This is the most important mechanic in the game — the moment a settlement transitions from a living, managed place into a permanent collectible card.

STABILITY SCORE

A settlement has a Stability Score (0–100). It rises when the settlement is running well and falls when it struggles. To seal a settlement, you must hold Stability ≥ 80 for a sustained "stability window."

stability_delta per tick: + 2.0 if food_surplus > 0 + 1.5 if warmth_sufficient + 1.0 per settler in "content" or "thriving" state + 0.5 per building at upgrade level 2+ − 3.0 if any settler is Hungry − 2.5 if warmth_insufficient − 5.0 per settler who has left (desertion event) − 1.0 if any job slot has been idle > 60s stability = clamp(stability + stability_delta, 0, 100) can_seal = (stability >= 80) AND (stability_window_timer >= seal_required_time)

THE SEAL CEREMONY

When conditions are met, a "Seal Settlement" button pulses with a golden glow. Pressing it triggers a short ceremony:

PHASE DURATION VISUAL
1 — Freeze 0.5s All settlers pause. Soft shimmer overlay on the map.
2 — Capture 1.0s Canvas zooms out and "photographs" the settlement — the card thumbnail is generated.
3 — Encase 1.5s The settlement shrinks into a card frame. Border colour reveals rarity.
4 — Fanfare 1.0s Rarity text appears. Pixel-art sparkle VFX. Card flips to show its sealed bonus.
5 — Collect — Card slides into the Collection panel. Settlement slot is now free for reuse.

SEALED BONUS GENERATION

The bonus a sealed settlement card provides is determined by the settlement's final state at the moment of sealing. Better-optimised settlements produce better bonuses.

sealed_bonus = { resource: dominant_produced_resource(settlement), rate: base_rate[tier] × efficiency_multiplier, efficiency: (avg_settler_happiness / 100) × (stability_at_seal / 100), special: trait_bonus_if_special_terrain_used(settlement) } // Example T1 outcomes: // Forest-heavy map + Leaf Rakers well-staffed → "+2 Fallen Leaves/min, +Forest tile bonus" // Fertile tile used + high food surplus → "+3 Food/min, +Fertile terrain in next founding"

Every sealed settlement becomes a card in the player's permanent collection. Cards are the game's collectible layer — they are objects with identity, rarity, and strategic value.

CARD RARITY

Rarity is determined at generation time based on a weighted roll modified by terrain composition and stability at sealing.

COMMON
55% chance
Standard grid
UNCOMMON
25% chance
+2 cells bonus
RARE
12% chance
+4 cells + special terrain
EPIC
6% chance
+6 cells + double bonus
LEGENDARY
2% chance
Unique tile + triple bonus

RARITY MODIFIERS

rarity_roll_modifier: + 15% if settlement had a Volcanic or Tundra tile + 10% if stability at seal was 95+ + 8% if all settlers were in "thriving" state at seal + 5% per special event survived during this settlement's lifetime + 3% per building at max upgrade level

CARD ANATOMY

Each card has the following data and visual elements:

ELEMENT DESCRIPTION
Thumbnail 16-colour pixel-art snapshot of the settlement at seal moment (64×48px)
Name Procedurally generated (terrain adjective + biome noun, e.g. "Misty Hollow")
Tier badge T1–T6 indicator in corner
Rarity frame Border style and colour reflect rarity
Grid size W×H shown as small numbers (affects founding tile arrangement)
Terrain bar Colour-coded strip showing terrain composition (e.g. 60% forest, 40% grass)
Sealed bonus Icon + value (e.g. 🍖 +3 Food/min)
Special trait One-line descriptor if rare+ (e.g. "Thriving Forest — Wood yield +20% in Hamlet founded here")

USE

11
IDLE & INCREMENTAL SYSTEMS

OFFLINE PROGRESS

When the player closes the game, the resource engine keeps running in a simplified form. Upon return, the player is shown an "While you were away..." summary: resources accumulated, any events that fired, population changes. Offline rate is 70% of online rate (incentive to stay engaged).

PASSIVE INCOME FROM SEALED CARDS

Every sealed card in the player's collection generates a trickle of its sealed bonus passively — even while the player is managing another settlement. This is the core idle loop:

passive_income_tick (every 60s): for each card in collection: if card.not_currently_used_in_founding: global_stockpile[card.bonus_resource] += card.bonus_rate × 0.1

PRESTIGE / RESET LAYER (T6 ONLY)

Upon sealing a Metropolis card, the player may optionally Prestige — resetting their active settlements but keeping their entire card collection and gaining a permanent multiplier to all future resource rates. This is the long-term replayability hook for the most dedicated players.

12
ROGUELITE LAYER

The roguelite element manifests as Settlement Events — random occurrences that challenge the player during the stabilisation phase of each settlement. They are not punishing enough to feel unfair, but meaningful enough to matter.

EVENT TYPES

TYPE TRIGGER EFFECT OUTCOME
🌧 Rainy Season T1–T3 −30% food production for 120s Survive: +10 rarity chance. Fail: −15 stability
🔥 Wildfire Forest tiles present 1 Forest tile converts to Ash (no yield) Survive: Ash tile becomes Fertile (reward)
🐺 Animal Migration T1–T2 Random building put out of service for 90s Reassign workers to compensate
🎉 Wanderer Arrives Any tier New settler joins with a random trait Free population boost
📦 Supply Cache Found Low stability +50 of a needed resource instantly Helpful catch-up mechanic
⚡ Harsh Winter T2+ Warmth demand ×2 for 180s Survive: Winter-hardy trait added to settlers
🧙 Travelling Merchant T3+ Offer: trade X resource for Y rare item Player choice — risk/reward
▶ ROGUELITE DESIGN NOTE
Events are the "narrative layer" of the game. They give each settlement a small story. A settlement called "Misty Hollow" that survived a Rainy Season has a character that a settlement that didn't can't have. This feeds into the collection fantasy — each card has a history.

14
CANVAS RENDERING

RENDERING PIPELINE

each frame: 1. ctx.clearRect(0, 0, W, H) 2. drawTerrain(settlement.grid) — bottom layer, terrain tiles 3. drawBuildingFootprints(buildings) — building base sprites 4. drawBuildingDetails(buildings) — upgrade-level details, smoke, fire 5. drawSettlers(settlers) — settler sprites, pathfinding dots 6. drawEffects(activeEffects) — warmth glow, event particles 7. drawUI_overlay() — resource bars, stability meter

TILE SYSTEM

All game sprites are drawn from a single sprite sheet (tileset). Each tile is 16×16px internally, rendered at 2× or 3× scale depending on screen size. This maintains the pixel-art look at all viewport sizes.

TILE_SIZE = 16 // internal pixel size SCALE = 3 // render scale (adjustable) CELL_SIZE = TILE_SIZE × SCALE = 48px on screen drawTile(ctx, tileX, tileY, cellX, cellY): src_x = tileX * TILE_SIZE src_y = tileY * TILE_SIZE dst_x = cellX * CELL_SIZE dst_y = cellY * CELL_SIZE ctx.drawImage(spriteSheet, src_x, src_y, TILE_SIZE, TILE_SIZE, dst_x, dst_y, CELL_SIZE, CELL_SIZE)

Use ctx.imageSmoothingEnabled = false to preserve crisp pixel edges at all scales. This is critical for the retro aesthetic.

SETTLER ANIMATION

Settlers have 4-frame walk animations (right, left, idle, work). Frames cycle every 200ms. Settlers have simple pathfinding: they walk to their assigned building and play the "work" animation there. Movement is cosmetic — it does not affect game logic.

▶ DEVELOPMENT PHILOSOPHY
Build the smallest thing that makes the core loop fun. The loop is: Place → Assign → Watch → Balance → Seal → Collect. If doing those 6 things once feels rewarding, the prototype is successful. Add everything else after.
